*The Tidal Covenant was the only faction in Hameria Ire that was not surprised when the world began to fail.*

*They had always known it would. That was, in a sense, the entire doctrine. You do not build a civilization around the understanding that all things change and then act startled when all things begin to change. They had been preparing for the Grand Harvest for eight thousand years, adapting their ships and their bodies and their theology to meet a great ending with grace rather than resistance.*

*What surprised them — what has made the current crisis feel, to the oldest of the Covenant's practitioners, like something personal — was the direction it came from.*

*They expected to be swept along by the current. They did not expect the current to stop.*

---

# Orym and the Way of Water

The Covenant does not resist. This is not passivity — it is a philosophy that required centuries to develop and requires discipline to maintain, the specific discipline of people who have learned, through the particular education of the sea, that the forces worth fighting are not the ones too large to stop.

They follow **Orym, the Aspect of Adaptation** — not a distant divine abstraction but an active presence that the Covenant's Current-Callers have been in direct conversation with since the first floating city learned to read the tides as instruction rather than obstacle. Orym is the water that takes the shape of whatever holds it. The survivor. The force that changes without losing itself, that flows around what it cannot break, that arrives at the same destination by paths that adapt to whatever the landscape has become.

The Covenant's core teaching is that identity is a river, not a stone. A stone holds its shape under pressure until it shatters. A river holds its *direction* under pressure, and finds a new path around every obstacle, and arrives eventually at the sea regardless of what was placed in its way. The question Orym asks — the question the Fragment of Adaptation asks, in its colder form — is not *what are you* but *what are you when the world takes away the shape you thought you were?*

For eight thousand years, the Covenant has been answering that question. Their bodies adapt to the pressure of deep water. Their trade-languages shift and absorb vocabulary from every port they touch. Their theology accommodates new understanding without discarding the core. They are, in the specific and deliberate sense, the most change-ready civilization in Hameria Ire — built to meet the Grand Harvest not with resistance but with the kind of acceptance that is stronger than resistance because it does not break under force.

The Iremael is not something they fear. They call it **the Great Tide**: a necessary force that clears old silt from the seabed so new reefs can form, that pushes dead growth off the hull so the vessel can move cleanly through the water. They understand that the Dimming was not cruelty. And they have prepared, across eight thousand years, to meet it.

# The Salted Stagnation

What is happening to the oceans was not supposed to happen.

The Binding required energy to establish. It requires energy to maintain. Fourteen centuries of continuous operation — holding the Iremael's great wheel stopped, sealing the Dimming, bleeding Serenitas through the Aetheric Well to fuel the Empire's golden civilization — has drawn on more than the imprisoned First Mother. It has drawn on the world itself, finding secondary sources for temporal momentum wherever the primary source falls short.

The oceans are one of those secondary sources.

The northern currents of the Great Bay have been slowing for three centuries, their waters thickening past what salt and cold can account for, developing a quality the Covenant's navigators noticed first as a sailing hazard and only later understood as something much worse. The word that emerged was **the Salted Stagnation**: a condition of hyper-density that is not freezing, is not poisoning, is not any natural maritime phenomenon the Covenant's long experience had provided a category for.

In Stagnation zones, time does not stop — but it moves at a different rate than the world outside. A ship that enters a dense zone and navigates through it for what feels like an hour may emerge to find a week has passed on the open water. The Depth-Singers, listening through the density with their sonar-magic, say the zones carry the sounds of previous moments — fragments of what happened in those waters decades ago, preserved in the thickened time like insects in amber. The Stagnation is not merely slowing the current. It is *accumulating the past*, because the past has nowhere to go.

Creatures caught in the deepest zones become what the Covenant calls **Salt-Statues** — held at the exact moment of their contact with the density, aware of everything happening around them but unable to move, unable to respond, suspended in a present that is moving too slowly for the body to function in but too quickly for the mind to forget. The Covenant has found them: sailors who entered Stagnation zones years ago, their eyes still tracking movement, their expressions still registering what they were doing when the density reached them. Still there. Still waiting.

This is the particular cruelty of the Stagnation — that it does not take. It *keeps*.

# Three Tides

The Covenant has navigated ideological conflict before. Adaptation, as a philosophy, accommodates disagreement — different approaches to the same problem are simply different shapes the river takes around the same obstacle. What the Stagnation has introduced is the possibility that some approaches lead to the waterfall, and the speed at which the disagreement is becoming irreversible.

## The Drifters
*The Traditionalists — Led by Current-Caller Nerida*

The Drifters are the beating heart of the Covenant's original tradition. Their response to the Stagnation is the same response the Covenant has given to every crisis in its history: *keep moving*. Chart the Stagnation zones. Learn their edges. Adapt the ships, adapt the bodies, adapt the routes. The current has not stopped entirely. There are still clean passages, still open water, still places where the density is thin enough to navigate. The Drifters are in those places, carrying trade and knowledge and people who need to be somewhere the Stagnation hasn't reached yet.

**Current-Caller Nerida's** deeper belief — the one she does not always say aloud but which underlies every navigational choice — is that the Stagnation is not the world's end. It is the world changing. The Grand Harvest is coming not as the gentle Dimming that was designed but as something harder, and the Covenant's role is not to stop it but to be moving when it arrives. The New Flow — whatever the world looks like on the other side of the Binding's collapse — will need people who remember how to read current. The Drifters intend to be those people.

## The Depth-Singers
*The Scholars — Led by Kaine Stormvoice*

**Kaine Stormvoice** has been listening to the Stagnation for twenty years and has gone partially translucent in the process — his skin carries the specific quality of deep-water creatures, light moving through him oddly, his eyes adapted to frequencies that require bone-shattering pressure to produce in normal water. He does not consider this a cost. He considers it data.

The Depth-Singers believe the Stagnation is not merely a symptom of the Binding's damage. They believe it is a *record* — that the temporal compression preserves within the dense water the accumulated memories of every Grand Harvest the Empire's crime has prevented. The sounds the Stagnation carries are not echoes. They are actual moments, held intact, waiting for someone to archive them. Kaine's work is to listen to those moments before the Stagnation's expansion closes the listening window — to extract from the captured past whatever information exists about what a completed Harvest looks like, how it moves, what it requires, what it leaves behind.

He does not know what he will do with this information when he has it. He knows it is worth having.

## The Anchors
*The Reactionaries — Led by Commodore Vax*

Commodore Vax's faction has done the thing the Covenant's doctrine most explicitly prohibits.

They have stopped.

Their floating cities are now chained to the seabed with iron — actual chains, ship-cable thickness, driven into the bay floor with the kind of engineering that belongs to fortification, not navigation. Their cities no longer drift. They no longer respond to the current. They hold position through anchored resistance, and **Commodore Vax** has described this as protection: the Stagnation advances, the Stagnation takes things that are moving, therefore stopping is survival.

The Depth-Singers have been trying to explain what they hear when they listen to the Stagnation zones around the chained cities. The Stagnation is not drawn to movement. It is drawn to stillness. The zones concentrate fastest around things that have stopped. The stationary cities are becoming nucleation points for the density, their fixed positions drawing the Stagnation toward them, their lack of current-response making them the most vulnerable locations in the entire bay.

Vax has been told this. He has heard it. He has looked at the open water where the Drifters operate — exposed, unprotected, always at the edge — and at his people who are afraid, and he has concluded that the fear of being swept away is worse than the thing that is coming to them instead.

It is not wisdom. But the Covenant, which was built on the understanding that every choice is an adaptation to something, has not yet found a way to tell him he is wrong without sounding like they have forgotten what it feels like to be afraid.

# Singing Through the Stagnation

Covenant magic works through resonance. The practitioner identifies the natural frequency of whatever they intend to affect — water, air, the specific vibration of a person's pain, the subtle hum of a ship's keel under pressure — and introduces a complementary frequency that changes its state. Not force. Not will imposed on the world. *Harmony*, the kind that finds the place where two notes together produce a third that neither could make alone.

The cost of this is transformation. You cannot sing a frequency you do not carry in yourself. The Current-Singer who shapes the sea takes on the sea's qualities — brine in the blood, the pressure-adaptation that belongs to deep things, occasionally the translucence that comes from spending too long at the frequency of the abyss. The transformation is not always permanent, but it is always real, and the Covenant has never pretended otherwise. You change the world by being changed by it. That is the doctrine in its most practical form.

What the Stagnation has done to this practice is introduce a problem the Covenant's tradition was not designed to address: some frequencies now require force the human body cannot safely generate.

The dense water does not respond to the harmonics that moved clean ocean. Its state is bound too tightly to the Empire's temporal mechanics for simple resonance to shift. The frequencies that can affect it are increasingly extreme — pitches that would never exist in natural water, harmonics that the Singer's bones were not built to carry. They shatter. Not occasionally, not as a consequence of error, but as the straightforward mechanical result of producing the frequency the Stagnation requires.

The Depth-Singers continue. They find ways to distribute the harmonic across multiple practitioners, to build up to the necessary frequency in stages, to use instruments rather than voice for the extremes. They adapt. But there is a range the Covenant has now reached — a density of Stagnation present in certain zones — where the adaptation required to sing through it is beyond what a human system can provide and still come back from.

They have not stopped listening. That is the Covenant's particular form of courage: continuing to attend to something that is becoming too large to address, because the alternative is not knowing what it is, and not knowing has never once made a wave smaller.
