*A world's geography is the record of what was done to it.*

*Hameria Ire was not always arranged like this — the golden towers concentrated in the center, the silver rot advancing from the east, the northern waters thickening toward stillness, the Emerald Reach absorbing what no one else will name. These are not accidents of climate or geology. They are consequences. The land wears its history the way people wear scars: not always visibly, not always legibly, but always precisely where the wound was made.*

*To travel through Hameria Ire is to read a story that no official archive will tell you. The story says: something happened here, fourteen centuries ago, and the world has been trying to process it ever since.*

# The Bonfire Keep

*At the edge of the world, past the point where Imperial maps run out and begin to speculate, there is a fire.*

It has been burning for fourteen hundred years. No one built it — or rather, it was not built the way cities are built, with intent and blueprint and the satisfaction of completion. The Bonfire Keep *arrived*, the way certain truths arrive: quietly, while everyone was looking elsewhere, and suddenly impossible to imagine the world without.

It sits in a fold of terrain that actively resists being remembered by anyone not meant to find it. The Thornspine Mountains rise on all sides, severe and permanent, and somewhere in their shelter — somewhere that Imperial cartographers have looked at and failed to see, again and again, across fourteen centuries of trying — is a structure that should not be comfortable and is. Low stone walls. A heavy door. Windows that glow amber from inside regardless of the hour. A smell, if the wind is right, of something burning that is not wood.

The fire at its center is called the **Heartflame**.

The Anchors will tell you, on the right nights, that the Keep does not exist entirely within the world's present moment. It sits, as Faeren has described it, *between the world's breaths* — in the small pause between the Iremael's great inhale and exhale, where the pressure of the cycle cannot quite reach. This is why the Stasis Glass that creeps through the mountain passes stops before the treeline. This is why the Silver Rot that threads through every forest to the west goes no further than the valley's edge. This is why ships' compasses, when their bearers are close enough, begin to point not toward magnetic north but toward the fire.

The Heartflame is keeping the pause from becoming permanent.

It is not a large fire, for something that holds the world in place. It fits in a hearth. It burns amber and occasionally deep blue, and it smells — this is the detail visitors mention most, after they have recovered enough to mention details — like something specific to each of them. Something from before. Something they did not know they had been missing.

It is fueled by stories. Not metaphorically. The Heartflame consumes the weight of genuine things: regret faced honestly, grief carried without collapsing, truth spoken when silence would have been easier. Every time someone sits before it and offers something real — not a performance of honesty, but the actual thing — the fire brightens. Every time a name fades from Ambric's blade, the Heartflame flares with the energy of a destiny completed.

If it goes out, the world does not end dramatically. It simply *stops*. The Stasis Glass will finish what the Salting started, and Hameria Ire will become a perfect, beautiful, unchanging thing that was once alive — suspended in a moment that will last until the universe finds another way to ask its question.

The Anchors understand this. They have understood it for a very long time. They tend the fire anyway.

## The Calling

The Heartflame does not wait for worthy people to find the Keep. It reaches.

Across every border and faction line, past Imperial censors and Clan fire-circles and the salt-spray of the Covenant's ports, it sends a pulse. Not a sound. Not a vision. A *resonance* — a frequency that finds the specific harmonic of certain souls and simply begins to hum. People who feel it describe it differently: a restlessness that sleep won't cure, dreams of a fire they've never seen, a persistent sense that somewhere, something is waiting for them to arrive.

The Anchors call this the Calling, and they are careful to say they do not send it. The Heartflame chooses. Or perhaps more accurately, it *recognizes*. It is looking for people with enough heat in them to matter — who have not yet been burned out by the world's particular cruelties, who are still capable of genuine surprise at injustice, who have not yet decided that the fight is someone else's problem.

When those people arrive at the Keep's door, Temmel is there to meet them. He will tell you this is coincidence. It is not coincidence. He has been standing near the door for a very long time.

## What to Find Here

The Keep is not large, but it is *deep* — in the specific way of places that contain more than their dimensions should allow. The common room holds the Heartflame and a number of tables that always seem to have enough chairs. The guest quarters above adapt, in small ways that visitors notice but cannot quite explain, to whatever the person sleeping in them most needs: a window facing east for someone who needs mornings, no window at all for someone who needs the dark, the smell of a specific place from a specific time for someone who needs to be reminded that something was once good.

Faeren keeps a library in a room that is reliably in a different location depending on which Faeren you follow and what you've told them you're looking for. It does not contain the Empire's sanitized history. It contains what Lady Celestis has been carefully losing for fourteen centuries, and what Faeren has been carefully preserving in the only place the censors cannot follow: a fire that answers to no Imperial charter.

The Heartflame itself is in the center of the common room, in a stone hearth that was clearly not designed for a fire this size. It burns anyway. It always has.

# Aurelios the Golden

*The city gleams. This is the first thing you notice and the last thing you forget — the way everything here catches the light and holds it, turns it golden, gives it back brighter than it arrived. Marble facades inlaid with hammered gold. Spires that catch the sunrise and scatter it into the streets below like something being given. The Great Roads running through the city's concentric rings with the mathematical precision of a civilization that believes order is a form of grace.*

*The second thing you notice, if you stay long enough, is that the gleam never stops. Not at dusk. Not at midnight. Not on overcast days when no sun reaches the streets at all. Aurelios the Golden is always lit, always warm, always producing more golden light than the sky alone could account for.*

*The third thing, if you know what you're looking at, is that the light comes from below.*

Aurelios is the Empire's capital and oldest city — the seat of every Emperor since the Founder, the origin point of the Great Roads that carry the Sunward civilization to every corner of Hameria Ire. It sits on the Golden Plains at the geographic center of Imperial territory, surrounded by the most productive agricultural land in the world, supplied by the most sophisticated distribution network in the world, defended by the most effective military in the world.

It is also built directly over a prison.

The **Aetheric Well** runs beneath the city's foundations — not a well in any architectural sense, but the mechanism through which Serenitas's siphoned essence rises from the depths, passes through the **Stasis Filtration Plant** on the city's western edge, and emerges as the golden mana that powers every casting hand in the Empire. The purple-red entropy that Serenitas bleeds, fed through the Filters, converted — this is what lights the city that never goes dark. This is what warms the forges, heals the sick in the Priesthood's hospitals, drives the magical academies that train the next generation of Imperial mages.

Most of those mages will never know what they are drawing on. The education is excellent. The history is clean.

## The Shape of Power

The city is arranged in concentric rings around the **Imperial Palace**, which occupies the highest point of the city's central hill. The palace is enormous — the accumulated ambition of twenty-six Emperors, each of whom added something, none of whom removed anything — and beneath its deepest foundations is the chamber where the siphoning apparatus is housed. Only the Emperor and certain members of the Twelve Witnesses have ever seen it. The Geas ensures that knowledge goes no further.

Below that: the binding chambers themselves, where Serenitas is held. No one who has ever been inside has described it in writing. There is no record of what it looks like, how it sounds, what the air does to you when you breathe it. There is only the fact of it — that beneath the most beautiful city in the world, in the dark below all the gold, something that was once the world's most beloved presence endures what was done to her.

The **Imperial Archives** occupy a district of their own near the palace: ten thousand years of civilizational record, organized, indexed, and maintained by Lady Celestis's office with extraordinary thoroughness. The gaps are exquisite. A sufficiently determined scholar can spend years in those halls and come away with a profound education in everything except the one thing that would make sense of it all. The connective tissue has been very carefully removed. What remains is not a lie, exactly. It is a true story with the ending cut out.

The **Stasis Filtration Plant** sits on the city's western edge, behind walls that are functional rather than decorative — the only significant structure in Aurelios that is not beautiful, because beauty was not the point when it was built. Archmage Vane manages its operations. The facility processes what the Aetheric Well produces into something the Empire's distribution network can carry. Visitors are not common. Workers do not discuss what the raw material looks like before the Filters change it.

## What the Citizens Know

They know they live in the greatest civilization in the world's history. They know the Golden Magic is Solara's gift, channeled through Aurelias the Founder's faith and maintained by the Solar Priesthood's devotion. They know that the Empire's light holds back the dark, that the borders have been defended for fourteen centuries, that their children receive educations that no other civilization in Hameria Ire can match.

All of this is true. None of it is the whole truth.

The people of Aurelios are not villains. They are inhabitants of an arrangement they did not make, sustained by an infrastructure they depend on, shaped by a faith that gives them genuine meaning. The court mage who conjures golden fire believes she is channeling the sun. The priest who heals with the Priesthood's light believes he is an instrument of Solara's grace. The soldier who guards the borders believes he is holding back the dark.

None of these things are entirely false.

# The Great Confluence

*The city moves. This is the first thing the Covenant's navigators will tell you: a city that does not move is a city that has stopped thinking. Cities have needs. The ocean has demands. The two negotiate constantly, and the vessel that understands the current is always faster than the vessel that tries to anchor through it.*

*The first thing they tell you now, with a quiet that they did not used to have: the negotiation is getting harder.*

The Great Confluence is not a city in the Imperial sense — fixed coordinates, permanent streets, the comfortable assumption that it will be in the same place next year as this year. It is a network of floating platforms, drydock-cities, and deep-water settlements that have been moving with the northern currents of the Great Bay for as long as the Tidal Covenant has existed. Its population of three hundred thousand sleeps on ships and pontoon-cities and underwater structures built around thermal vents, because they learned early that the only ground worth standing on is ground that understands change.

The northern bay's currents are no longer what they were.

## The Salted Stagnation

Three centuries ago, the Covenant's navigators noticed something. The northern currents were slowing — not in the seasonal rhythms they had charted for thousands of years, but in a deeper, stranger way. The water was thickening. Not freezing — the temperature was unchanged. Not poisoned — nothing biological was responding to it. Just *denser*, in a way that had no natural precedent and resisted every category the Covenant's long experience had built.

The **Salted Stagnation** is what happens when a civilization borrows temporal momentum from the world's oceans and does not pay it back. The Binding requires more energy than Serenitas alone can provide; the world is its secondary source, and the ocean is vast enough that the borrowing, for centuries, looked like fluctuation. It no longer looks like fluctuation.

In Stagnation zones, time does not stop — but it moves at a different rate than the world outside. A ship that enters a dense zone and navigates it for what feels like an hour may emerge to find a week has passed on the open water. Creatures caught in the deepest zones become **Salt-Statues**: held at the exact moment of contact with the density, aware of everything happening around them, unable to move or respond, suspended in a present that is moving too slowly for the body to function in but too quickly for the mind to forget. The Covenant has found them. Sailors who entered Stagnation zones years ago, their eyes still tracking, their expressions still registering the moment they were in when the density reached them. Still there. Still waiting.

The Stagnation does not take. It *keeps*.

## The Glass Docks and the Abyssal Shelf

The **Glass Docks** on the Confluence's outer edge are where ships first enter zones where the water has begun to crystallize — not into ice, but into something clearer, denser, with the specific quality of amber rather than glass. Ships that moor there too long emerge with their hulls filmed in a faint translucence that the Depth-Singers can read like growth rings, measuring how much time the vessel spent in the zone by how deep the film has penetrated the wood.

The **Abyssal Shelf** is the deep canyon at the heart of the bay where the currents pile up — where the slowing water has nowhere to go and accumulates, year by year, in concentrations that the Depth-Singers who listen to them say carry actual moments preserved from decades past. Kaine Stormvoice has been listening there for twenty years. He has gone partially translucent in the process. He considers this data.

Three factions navigate these waters. The **Drifters** keep moving, charting the edges of the Stagnation zones, carrying trade and knowledge and people who need to be somewhere the density hasn't reached. The **Depth-Singers** listen to what the Stagnation preserves. The **Anchors** have chained their floating cities to the seabed — a decision that every Depth-Singer in the bay has tried to explain is accelerating exactly what Commodore Vax believes it is preventing. The Stagnation concentrates fastest around things that have stopped.

Vax has been told this. He has looked at his people, who are afraid. He continues to hold position.

# The Emerald Reach

*The forest does not look sick at first. This is the thing that stays with travelers who have not been warned: the Emerald Reach is still beautiful. The canopy still holds its green. The mycelial paths still glow faintly blue in the evenings where the druids have walked them for generations. The grove-cities in the high branches still move with wind and weather, still creak in the particular language of living wood.*

*Look east. Look at where the treeline meets the morning light.*

*The leaves there are silver. Not autumn silver — not the warm, temporary gold of a season turning. A cold, permanent silver, the color of hammered metal, of things that have stopped in the middle of becoming.*

*Stand quiet enough, and you will hear what the druids hear: a sound at the exact edge of perception, made of a million small voices saying the same thing in different languages. Something that amounts to* stop.

The Emerald Reach is the oldest continuous civilization in Hameria Ire — older than the Empire, older than the Covenant, old enough that the grove-network threading through its roots carries communication in a language that predates anything humans have written down. The Verdant Clans live here by the doctrine of Thymal, the Aspect of Growth: that nothing grows forever in one direction, that change requires loss, that loss is not the enemy of life but the mechanism by which life makes room for itself.

They have been watching the eastern edge silver for three centuries. They know what it means. They refuse to call it something else to be polite.

## Vaelora, the Mother-Tree

At the Reach's center stands **Vaelora**, the Mother-Tree — ancient beyond the ability of any living thing to accurately measure, her canopy large enough to create its own microclimate, her root system extending so deep and so far that every connected grove in the Reach is, in some sense, her. The Grove-Speakers put their hands to her bark each morning and listen for the Root's voice beneath the Silver Static.

Vaelora is not dying. This is both the miracle and the horror. The Silver Rot does not kill — it *stops*. The trees at the eastern edge are not dead. They are perfect. Every leaf retains its shape and color and veining in hammered glass. The sap inside has thickened to a metallic slowness. The wood has hardened past what wood should be. Stand in a grove that has been fully taken and you are standing in the most extraordinary art anyone has ever made, and nothing in it will ever grow again.

Vaelora has been absorbing the Rot for fourteen centuries because living systems try to process what the wind brings them. The Stasis Ash that falls from Aurelios's golden towers follows the wind lines southwest into the Reach, and the Mother-Tree and every connected grove have been trying, for over a millennium, to integrate something fundamentally opposed to the act of living. The Silver Static that runs through the mycelial web is the sound of that effort at the edge of failure.

Every druid in the Reach has silver in their veins. Every elder carries it in their face — a particular tiredness that is not fatigue but accumulation, the look of people who have been absorbing the world's weight for so long they have forgotten what unweighted feels like.

They are not broken by it. They choose, every morning, to keep tending.

## Three Factions, One Question

The Rot has not broken the Clans. It has broken them apart — not along clean lines of territory or generation, but along the question of what it means to tend a living thing that is losing its ability to live.

**Theron** leads the **Grove-Speakers**: patience, not the passive kind, but the patience of people who understand that a wound opened across fourteen centuries cannot be closed in an afternoon. They believe the Mother-Tree is vast and old enough to find a process for the Empire's waste, given the right conditions and enough time. They tend the alliance. They maintain the peace that keeps the Clans intact.

**Celia** leads the **Rootwardens**: triage. She has fought the infected edge for thirty years and carries silver from elbow to shoulder as a souvenir of the day she held a grove long enough for her people to evacuate. The Rootwardens cut what cannot be saved. They do not ask whether the network can digest the Rot. They ask how much can be cut before what remains is no longer the Reach they were built to protect.

The **Bramble-King** leads the **Thorns**, and will not give a name. They began as Rootwardens who reached a conclusion the others refused: the infection's source is not within the Reach, and pruning will not cure a wound that is still being made. The Siphon-Stations that relay Imperial magic across its distribution network manufacture Stasis Ash with every casting cycle. As long as they operate, the Reach will continue to receive what they exhale.

The Thorns have been destroying Siphon-Stations. They call it pruning at the source. They have also begun weaponizing the Silver Rot itself — infected wood, carefully harvested, carried to Imperial agricultural territories, introduced into the soil. *Show them the weight of their own light*, the Bramble-King has said. *Let them understand what they have been asking the world to carry in their name.*

# Concordia Stellaris

*Every seven years, the twin moons Thaelis and Morwyn align with mathematical precision that the Covenant's astronomers have charted back further than any other civilization's records reach. For three nights, their combined light is bright enough to cast shadows at midnight, to silver the grass of the plain where they rise together, to make the stars look faint by comparison.*

*Three hundred years ago, someone decided that a world this divided needed, at minimum, a place where the divisions could be briefly set aside. Concordia Stellaris was built on that plain. Not by any single faction — which is the point, which has always been the point.*

Concordia Stellaris is a neutral city — or rather, a city that becomes itself every seven years and maintains a skeleton the rest of the time. Its permanent population of two thousand swells to fifty thousand during the Harvest Festival: merchants and diplomats and scholars and pilgrims and the specific category of person who travels long distances not because they need anything at the destination but because they need to be somewhere that is not where they started.

The city has no standing army. It has a festival council, a set of agreements that every major power has signed regarding behavior within its boundaries, and the specific protection of being too useful to any single faction for any single faction to risk destroying it.

## The Alignment and the Festival

The **Harvest Festival** is not, despite its name, primarily a harvest celebration. It is a negotiation — a structured opportunity for the three great powers to sit across from each other in a place where the normal rules of political posturing are relaxed by mutual agreement, because the alternative is conducting diplomacy through intermediaries who have their own interests and the festival provides fewer opportunities for convenient misunderstanding.

It is also a market, a pilgrimage, a academic conference, and the single most significant gathering of people from every faction in Hameria Ire that occurs anywhere in any seven-year span. The **Wandering Markets** treat it as their most important event of the cycle. Information that has been moving in pieces across the continent tends to find its complete form here, in conversations that happen in wine tents at the second hour after midnight.

The twin moons rise over a plain that was neutral before any of the three powers existed. The festival charter requires that no faction's banners fly within the city's central quarter during the alignment — only the white-and-silver festival flags, which belong to no one and therefore to everyone. On the three nights of the alignment, there is light bright enough to read by, a silence in the middle of the crowds that descends when the moons reach their peak, and people who have been enemies for decades standing in the same silver light, looking up.

No one has resolved the world's central crime at Concordia Stellaris. But things have been said here, in the margin between one seven-year cycle and the next, that could not have been said anywhere else. And what is said, in the right ear, in the right moment, under the right light — that, Faeren would tell you, is exactly how the truth survives when everything else is trying to bury it.
