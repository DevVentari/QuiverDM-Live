# The Hunt Begins

*Characters of level 11. At the heart of a desert where time has stopped moving forward, a man who destroyed his civilization through the perfection of his convictions has been waiting ten millennia for someone to tell him he was wrong.*

---

## Background

The Sunward Wastes are what remains when an empire decides that control of the present is insufficient and reaches for the past and future simultaneously. The civilization that stood here at the start of the previous Grand Harvest was, by every surviving account, extraordinary — its architecture, its scholarship, its administrative precision, the sheer sustained ambition of the project it undertook. The **Last Emperor** was not a cruel man in the ordinary sense. He was a man who believed, with the complete and unironic sincerity that only the very intelligent and very powerful can sustain, that perfect order was the same thing as perfect goodness. That if you could stop entropy — slow it, contain it, reduce its variable reach over human lives — you would have done the most important thing a civilization could do.

He was wrong. Not wrong in the way of a tyrant who knew the cost and paid it with someone else's suffering. Wrong in the way of someone who genuinely could not see what perfect order cost the people ordered, because the people ordered had learned, over generations, not to let him see it. The catastrophe that ended his civilization at the close of that cosmic cycle was not external. It was the accumulated weight of everything his system had optimized away pressing back through the only channel left to it. The ending was absolute. The Wastes are what the absolute ending looks like, crystallized and preserved and shimmering with the light of moments that never finished happening.

He is still here. Not alive — preserved, the way an argument is preserved when no one is left to answer it. The **Fragment of Command** floats above the throne of the **Crystal Palace** because the Fragment of Command is, in some deep structural sense, his. It was crystallized from the same impulse that built his empire, and it will not be claimed by force. It can only be claimed by someone who can show him — not tell him, not argue him into it, but *show him* through the quality of their engagement — that the lesson his civilization paid for with its existence can be learned without paying that cost again.

This is not a dungeon crawl with a philosophical veneer. The trials are the point. The combat is what fills the spaces between the trials and tests whether the party is the kind of people whose philosophical arguments mean anything. The **Echo of the Last Emperor** has had ten thousand years to construct the most rigorous version of his own position, and he will not be impressed by surface-level counterarguments. He will be moved — finally, after ten thousand years — by the specific thing that could only come from people who have done the work.

The party who defeats him philosophically does not win. The party who redeems him wins. The distinction is everything.

---

## Adventure Hooks

**The Alliance's Decision:** The war council chose the Sunward Wastes. Whatever combination of mediation and political pressure produced the unanimous decision, the faction leaders are agreed: the first fragment is **Command**, and the party is the expedition. **Theron** provides what the Verdant Circle's long memory has preserved about the Wastes — impressions rather than maps, sensory data from the last Verdant emissary who entered and returned, four thousand years ago. She described it as *a place that is very interested in you*.

**Lyria's Private Briefing:** Before departure, **High Sage Lyria** requests a private word with the party. What she shares is not in the official expedition dossier: twenty-two years ago, the Imperial court conducted a classified research expedition into the edges of the Wastes. Three scholars entered. Two returned, both changed in ways they could not fully articulate. One left a journal. Lyria has the journal. She has been carrying it since the night of the Heartflame ceremony, waiting for this specific moment to give it away. The journal's final entry reads: *He is not guarding the Fragment. He is waiting to be understood. We were not the right people. I do not know who the right people are, but I believe they exist.*

**The Fragment's Resonance:** As the expedition approaches the boundary of the Wastes, the party's cosmic resonance — whatever form it has taken in their own bodies since the ceremony — responds. Not painfully. More like recognition. Like something in the Fragment recognizing something in them, across the distance, and making a small adjustment to its posture. Characters with sensitivity to magic or cosmic forces may receive, briefly and without clear meaning, an image: a man on a throne with a crown fused to his skull, and the specific expression of someone who has been rehearsing a very long apology.

## Key Features

### The Temporal Wasteland

*Three-day journey across crystallized time*

The desert does not look the way deserts look. The sand, where it appears, is glass — or rather, the distinction between sand and glass has become academic here, because the heat that made the glass never left. The crystal formations that rise from the surface are not geological. They are temporal: frozen moments, solidified instants, the particular amber-gold light of time that was moving and stopped. Walking through the Wastes means walking through the residue of a civilization's final hours, preserved at the scale of geography.

The temporal storms are the primary hazard. They move like weather but respond like thought — they are attracted to decision-making, to moments of significant choice, and the party will draw them simply by being the kind of people who make significant choices. At the start of each in-game hour of travel, roll d8 and consult the following effects:

- **1–2:** Double-speed zone. Movement feels frictionless. Time passes at twice the normal rate for anything inside the zone; outside, hours become minutes. Rations deplete at double speed. *The storm does not feel malicious. It feels like enthusiasm — the enthusiasm of a moment that wanted to matter and never got to.*
- **3–4:** Decelerated field. The air becomes thick with unspent time. Difficult terrain that is not physical. DC 12 Constitution saves to avoid one level of exhaustion from the effort of moving through moments that do not want to proceed.
- **5–6:** Memory bleed. The environment shows them something from the empire's history — not a vision exactly, more like the landscape briefly remembering what it looked like when it was inhabited. Citizens going about their days. Architecture still standing. The mundane life of a civilization that did not know it was about to end. DC 14 Wisdom save or become distracted (disadvantage on Perception) for an hour.
- **7:** Chrono fracture. A split in time opens. The party can see two versions of the path ahead simultaneously — one from before the catastrophe, one from after. Navigation requires choosing which version to follow; following the wrong one adds 1d4 hours to the journey.
- **8:** Clear. The storm has passed or has not yet arrived. The light is beautiful in the specific way that only the light in places that have suffered deeply is beautiful.

DC 15 Survival checks are required daily to navigate reliably; failure adds 4 hours to travel time. During active temporal storms, DC 12 Constitution saves must be made or the character gains one level of exhaustion from temporal exposure.

**Day One** brings Temporal Wraith patrols — bureaucratic spirits drifting along paths that no longer correspond to any existing road network, processing administrative functions for a population that no longer exists. A crystallized Imperial waystation stands at the day's midpoint: the building intact, the contents suspended, a meal still on the table in mid-preparation, the cook's expression still carrying the beginning of a thought she never finished. Characters who spend time in the waystation may find provisions (if they can be carefully extracted from the temporal suspension) and a better understanding of what ordinary life looked like in the empire's shadow.

**Day Two** brings Crystal Golem guardians drifting in pairs across the desert floor — order constructs still executing the last commands they received, which were commands about maintaining the boundaries of an empire that no longer exists. They do not attack immediately; they challenge. The challenge is formal and follows specific protocols. Characters who engage with the protocols rather than ignoring them learn something about the culture that made them. The ruins visible on the horizon are the empire's last settlements — not destroyed but *completed*, every architectural intention fulfilled and then stopped.

**Day Three** brings the full weight of temporal instability. The boundary between past and present becomes permeable. Echo visions of Imperial citizens manifest and hold brief, startled conversations with the party before dissipating — they do not know they are echoes, and the party's presence registers to them as unusual rather than impossible. A character who spends more than a few minutes in sustained conversation with an echo risks DC 16 Wisdom save or becomes temporally anchored (restrained, as the timeline briefly treats them as part of the past) for 1d4 rounds.

### The Crystal Palace Approach

*Palace exterior and grounds*

The palace is visible from a full day's travel away, which was probably intentional. The original construction was marble and gold, extravagant and certain of itself, the architecture of a civilization that believed permanence was achievable. What the temporal crystallization has done to it is not damage. It is something closer to translation — the same statement made in a different material. The spires twist in ways that marble should not permit. The surfaces reflect not light but *echoes of everyone who ever approached*, a shimmer of intention and history compressed into the stone's new form.

The approaches to the palace are guarded by forces that are not hostile in the conventional sense. The **Temporal Wraiths** near the gate are bureaucratic spirits performing eternal administrative loops — they will challenge entry formally and follow challenge protocols to the letter. The **Crystal Golems** stationed at the flanks represent the principle of order as its own justification and will attack what they perceive as chaotic energy (sudden movement, unannounced magical effects, obvious preparation for violence). The **Echo Guards** at the palace doors are phantom Imperial soldiers still executing their last standing orders, which were to admit credentialed visitors through the proper process.

The party has four approach options, each of which teaches them something about the empire's logic before they enter it:

- **Main Gates:** Direct entry through the formal challenge process. The Wraiths will accept credentials (any official document, any statement of purpose made with evident sincerity). Time-consuming, but teaches the party how the empire understood itself.
- **Service Tunnels:** Available to characters who examine the palace perimeter. The tunnels predate the crystallization and are temporally unstable — navigation requires DC 15 Intelligence checks hourly. Bypasses the formal challenge but misses the context the challenge would provide.
- **Temporal Rift:** A crack in the palace's crystallized exterior, visible to characters with arcane sensitivity, that leads directly to an interior corridor. DC 20 Arcana check to navigate safely; failure causes 4d10 temporal damage (the character briefly exists in two points in time simultaneously, which is not comfortable) and deposits them in a random room.
- **Diplomatic Entry:** Announcing the party's purpose formally — as Pregenitors come to claim the Fragment for the current cycle, in accordance with the protocols of cosmic succession. This requires knowledge of those protocols (or confident improvisation of them) and DC 15 Persuasion. Success opens the gates without challenge; the Wraiths recognize the designation and stand aside with something that looks, in their spectral way, like relief.

### The Hall of Eternal Orders

*First major chamber of the Crystal Palace*

The hall is enormous and full of purposeful activity, which is the worst thing about it. Translucent figures in Imperial administrative dress move through the space in precise patterns — receiving documents, processing them, filing them, retrieving them — for an empire that ended ten thousand years ago. The paperwork they handle is real: requests for resource allocation, census records, infrastructure reports, all of it for cities that are glass and silence now, all of it processed with complete professional seriousness.

Four to six **Temporal Wraiths** perform eternal bureaucratic functions here. They are not malicious. They are profoundly, heartbreakingly committed. Any creature that lingers in the hall must make a DC 15 Wisdom saving throw each minute or be compelled to join the process — they will sit at a vacant desk and begin processing paperwork, and the work will feel urgent and important, which is how they will know something has gone wrong. A character successfully extracted from compulsion can describe the document they were processing, which will be a genuine Imperial record and may contain useful information about the palace's layout, the emperor's history, or the nature of the Fragment.

The Wraiths cannot be permanently destroyed by conventional combat — they reform within the bureaucratic loop. The hall can be cleared through three approaches:

- **Bureaucratic Mastery:** A character who engages the system on its own terms — who actually processes a full document according to Imperial protocol (DC 18 Investigation to understand the system, DC 15 Persuasion to submit a countermanding order through proper channels) — can introduce a procedural interruption that breaks the loop for 1 hour.
- **Temporal Disruption:** DC 20 Arcana to identify the specific resonance maintaining the loop and introduce sufficient dissonance to collapse it. Destroys the loop permanently and allows the Wraiths to finally stop. *The moment they stop — when the last one sets down its papers and goes still — the hall is very quiet.*
- **Imperial Authority:** Characters who entered through Diplomatic Entry, or who can demonstrate legitimate cosmic succession credentials, may simply issue an order countermanding the Wraiths' standing instructions. DC 12 Intimidation. The Wraiths will comply and stand down, because compliance with proper authority was, literally, their entire purpose.

The lesson this room teaches — the one the **Echo of the Last Emperor** built it to teach, even if he did not know that was what he was doing — is that authority must serve people, not perpetuate systems.

### The Throne Room Gauntlet

*Antechamber to the throne room*

Two **Crystal Golems** stand in the antechamber, twelve feet tall, their crystalline bodies refracting the ambient temporal light into precise geometric patterns on the floor and walls. They have been here for ten thousand years and they are not bored; they do not experience time the way organic beings experience it. They experience it as an accumulation of opportunities to execute their purpose.

Their purpose is to test whether what approaches the throne is worthy of the throne. Worthiness, in their programming, means discipline. They cannot attack creatures that demonstrate perfect coordination and self-command — characters who move deliberately, who have clearly communicated intention before acting, who present as a coherent unit rather than a collection of individual impulses. They regenerate 10 hit points per turn while inside the palace, because the palace sustains them, and they cannot be outrun (the antechamber has no alternate exit).

The real question the Golems pose is the same one the Hall of Eternal Orders posed from the other direction: they represent the temptation to substitute control for cooperation. A party that defeats them through pure discipline will find the experience unsatisfying in a way they might not be able to articulate. A party that defeats them by demonstrating cooperative trust — covering each other, communicating openly, functioning as a unit that chooses to coordinate rather than a unit that is commanded to — will find that the Golems step aside before they are fully defeated, recognizing the quality they were built to look for.

## The Echo of the Last Emperor

*Central throne room*

The throne room is pure. That is the only word for it — pure in the way that something becomes pure when every impurity has been removed by force. The floor is a single unbroken plane of crystallized temporal glass. The walls are symmetrical to the degree that architectural symmetry implies effort. The light comes from everywhere and casts no shadows. The Emperor sits on the throne at the room's center, and the crown has fused to his skull with temporal crystal over ten thousand years, and his eyes are burning with absolute certainty.

He is preserved at the moment of his greatest failure. He knows this. This is, perhaps, the most important thing about him — he is not confused about what happened. He watched his civilization end. He understood, in that moment, what his pursuit of perfect order had cost. The certainty in his eyes is not the certainty of someone who believes he was right. It is the certainty of someone who has spent ten thousand years in full knowledge of what he got wrong, unable to change, unable to make it right, unable to do anything except wait for someone to arrive who might be able to carry the understanding he could not.

*He looks at the party the way someone looks at people they have been waiting for without knowing if they would come.*

Combat is possible. The **Echo of the Last Emperor** has CR 13 statistics, legendary resistance, and the will to use them — not because he wants to defeat the party, but because the Fragment of Command cannot be held by people who cannot hold their own against what it demands of them. If combat is the only mode the party engages in, they will win, eventually, and the Fragment will become claimable, and the Echo will dissipate, and they will leave with a magical item they do not fully understand. This is the minimum success condition. The room will feel empty afterward in a way that has nothing to do with the absence of an enemy.

The full success condition requires three rounds of philosophical debate, interspersed with whatever combat proves necessary, and it requires genuine engagement rather than performance.

**Round One: The Nature of Order.** *"I built what I believed would protect them. I removed every variable that could cause them suffering. I believed — I knew — that if I could stop the chaos, the loss, the unpredictability that tears lives apart, I would have given them the greatest possible gift. Tell me where I was wrong."* The DC is 18 Persuasion, but the number is not the point. What works is an argument that takes his premise seriously before challenging it — that acknowledges what he was trying to do, that honors the genuine care that motivated the project, before addressing what the project cost. Arguments that begin with his wrongness will fail. Arguments that begin with his love for his people have a chance.

**Round Two: The Price of Control.** *"They did not tell me. In forty years of rule, no citizen told me directly that they were suffering under my order. I built every system of accountability I could conceive of, and none of them delivered the truth. If they had simply told me—"* The DC is 20 Insight, and the insight required is genuine: he needs to understand not that his people suffered but *why they did not tell him*. Why systems of accountability built by the person who needs accountability cannot, structurally, deliver accountability. Why love, even the deep and real love he had for his civilization, is not sufficient if the structures it builds do not permit the loved to speak. Characters with personal experience of systems that protected their builders — from any background, any history — can draw on that experience here. The Emperor will recognize authenticity. He has had ten thousand years to develop his sensitivity to it.

**Round Three: The Personal Argument.** *"You have argued well. But you have argued theory. Tell me something true about how you lead."* This round requires each party member to offer something specific and personal — a choice they made that they are not sure was right, a time they held authority and felt its weight, a moment when cooperation required surrendering control they wanted to keep. The Emperor is not evaluating correctness. He is evaluating honesty. The DC is 15 for each character, but a character who offers something genuinely personal, something with actual vulnerability in it, succeeds automatically.

**Winning Two of Three Rounds,** plus the moment of genuine remorse the party's approach has required — *they must show, not perform, genuine acknowledgment of his suffering, of what it is to have been right about caring and wrong about method, to have paid the full price of a sincere mistake at civilization scale* — releases the Fragment. The crystal around the Emperor's crown begins to dissolve. Not quickly. The dissolution takes several minutes, and during those minutes he speaks — not the formal debate of the trials but something else, something that has been waiting ten thousand years for someone to hear it.

*He tells them what he wished he had understood. Not as lesson, not as warning. As the final thought of someone who has finished.*

When the dissolution completes, the room is empty. The Fragment of Command rests where the throne was, hovering slightly above the floor, and the air has the quality of a space where something difficult has been accomplished.

## Consequences

**Full Success:** The Fragment is claimed through the redemption of the Echo — through genuine engagement with his tragedy rather than the performance of an argument. The Echo finds peace after ten millennia of temporal imprisonment, and the party carries that understanding forward: not just the mechanical benefits of the Fragment but a deep and functional comprehension of what leadership costs and what it is for. The cosmic awareness the Fragment confers provides complete intelligence on the remaining fragment sites, the corruption timeline, and the nature of Pregenitor purpose. Future fragment guardians, learning of this success through channels the party does not yet understand, will approach them as people worth testing rather than obstacles worth defeating.

**Minimum Success:** The Fragment is claimed through combat after a failed or unengaged philosophical trial. The Echo dissipates without peace. The party understands, in the hollow quality of their victory, that they won something and lost something in the same moment. The Fragment's power is fully functional, but the deeper knowledge it might have conferred is absent. The alliance's confidence in the party holds. The party's confidence in themselves is a more complicated question.

**Failure:** The party is lost to temporal hazards, defeated in combat, or — most costly — philosophically defeated by the Echo. Not because he is invincible but because he asked genuine questions and received only the shape of answers. The Fragment remains unclaimed. The alliance's confidence fractures. The corruption timeline does not pause for the expedition's recovery. The party leaves the Wastes knowing that what they faced was not a monster but a man, and that they were not yet equal to what he needed from them.
